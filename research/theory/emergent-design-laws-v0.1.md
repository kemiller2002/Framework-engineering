# Emergent Design Laws v0.1

Status: Provisional research synthesis

These are not constitutional principles.

They are evidence-grounded design tendencies emerging from FE-008, FE-011A, FE-012A, and FE-012B.

## 1. Bound Scope Before Reasoning

Reasoning improves when the analyst or framework first fixes the problem boundary, target system, or decision scope.

Current basis:

- FE-012A extraction patterns
- FE-012B synthesis patterns
- multiple FE-011A redesigned outputs

## 2. Separate Observation From Interpretation

Frameworks become easier to audit when raw observations are kept distinct from what those observations are taken to mean.

Current basis:

- FE-011A strongest redesigned outputs
- FE-012A reconstruction strength in reasoning-heavy artifacts

## 3. Preserve Uncertainty

Frameworks should not imply certainty that the evidence does not support.

Current basis:

- FE-011A comparative output pattern
- prior calibration and appropriate-precision work

## 4. Preserve Alternatives Until Evidence Narrows Them

Premature closure weakens diagnostic and evaluative reasoning.

Current basis:

- FE-011A Five Whys comparison
- FE-012A diagnostic artifact reconstructions

## 5. Verify Before Closure

Reasoning artifacts are more robust when they include some explicit verification move before final commitment.

Current basis:

- FE-011A traceability pattern
- FE-012A and FE-012B repeated reuse of `Verify`

## 6. Add Reassessment Triggers In Dynamic Contexts

When the environment changes quickly, a framework should specify when prior judgments should be reopened.

Current basis:

- FE-011A OODA comparison
- FE-012A adaptive artifact patterns
- FE-012B synthesis pressure in dynamic problems

## 7. Add Only Enough Structure To Solve The Identified Weakness

More structure can improve reasoning quality, but it also raises complexity and overhead.

Current basis:

- FE-011A increased traceability with increased complexity
- FE-012B heavier syntheses in multi-control problems

## Open Caution

These laws may not be universal.

They may hold more strongly for reasoning-heavy frameworks than for execution-heavy or highly representational artifacts.
